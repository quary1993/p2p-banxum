Part 1

BANXUM P2P Primer: what you need to know before you touch a screen

1. What BANXUM is

BANXUM is a peer-to-peer (P2P) lending marketplace. "Peer-to-peer" means individual people lend money directly to businesses, instead of a bank sitting in the middle and lending its own money.

Here is the basic idea. Individual investors (we also call them "lenders") put money into BANXUM. That money is then used to fund loans to vetted business borrowers. The borrowers pay the money back over time, with interest. The interest is shared back to the investors who funded that loan. Investors can later take their money out again.

BANXUM is operated by Garanta Finanzgruppe AG, a Swiss financial group. "Garanta" is the operator, and you, as an operations admin, work for Garanta. BANXUM does not lend its own money and is not a bank. It runs the marketplace, checks who everyone is, moves money correctly, and keeps records.

This manual is about the internal ops console served at /admin, which only Garanta staff use. It is NOT the app that investors log into. Throughout this manual, "the console" or "the admin console" always means the staff tool, never the investor app.

2. The four people in the story

Almost everything in BANXUM involves these four parties. Learn them first; the rest of the manual refers to them constantly.

  • Investor (lender): an individual who deposits money and funds loans to earn interest. In the system an investor account has an account_type of either "natural_person_lender" (a real human investing for themselves) or "legal_entity_lender_representative" (a person acting on behalf of a company that invests). Every lender account is given a short unique investor_reference code when it is created. Investors log into the investor app themselves; they do not use the admin console.
  • Borrower: a company that receives a loan and pays it back with interest. A borrower is NEVER a login account. It is a record (a "BorrowerEntity") that you, the admin, create and maintain by hand. The borrower has no portal, no password, and never logs in anywhere. Borrowers are vetted through KYB (explained in section 7) before they can receive money.
  • Garanta operator (you): a Garanta staff member working in the admin console. There are two staff account types: "admin" (normal operations staff) and "superadmin" (staff who can also change platform-wide settings and create other admins). Note that the account type alone is not enough: admin powers also depend on the account's is_staff flag being set, and superadmin powers additionally require the is_superuser flag, so a "superadmin" account type that lacks is_superuser would not pass superadmin checks. You enter borrowers and loans, review compliance, confirm bank movements, and run the day-to-day operation.
  • Didit: an external identity-verification company. "External" means it is a third-party provider, not part of Garanta. When a person needs their identity checked, BANXUM sends them to Didit. Didit runs the identity and anti-money-laundering checks and reports the result back. The default identity provider on every verification case is "didit".

3. How money moves

Money flows in one big cycle. Follow it once and most screens will make sense.

Step 1, the investor sends a bank transfer. The investor wires money from their own bank to a Garanta collection bank account. "Collection account" just means the Garanta-controlled bank account that receives investor money.

Step 2, the transfer is matched and ledgered. When the money arrives, an admin confirms the bank operation and records it in the ledger (the system's official books). Crucially, the investor must put a specific payment reference on their bank transfer so we can tell whose money it is. That reference has the exact format BX-{currency}-{investor_reference}, for example BX-CHF-AB12CD for a Swiss-franc deposit from investor AB12CD. The reference is unique per investor and per currency and must be copied unchanged into the transfer description. A transfer with no reference, or the wrong one, cannot be matched automatically and lands in a suspense/unmatched state for an admin to resolve.

Step 3, the money becomes an investable balance. Once matched, the deposit creates a "balance lot" (think of it as a dated parcel of that investor's money) and the amount becomes investable. Balance buckets are explained in section 5.

Step 4, the investor invests in a loan (the primary market). The investor places an investment order against a published loan. Their investable balance is reserved for that loan. When the loan finishes funding, the reserved money is committed to it.

Step 5, the borrower is disbursed. Once a loan is fully funded and the borrower's KYB is approved, an admin pays out (disburses) the loan principal to the borrower's bank account.

Step 6, the borrower repays. Over the loan term the borrower sends repayments (principal plus interest) back to a Garanta collection account. An admin records each repayment and distributes it to the investors who funded that loan, in proportion to how much each one put in.

Step 7, returns land back in the investor's balance. Each investor's share of principal and interest is credited back to their BANXUM balance as new balance lots.

Step 8, the investor withdraws to their bank. When the investor wants their money out, they request a withdrawal to a registered bank account (a "payout instruction"). An admin finalises it and the money is wired from the Garanta collection account back to the investor's bank.

  • Deposit reference format: BX-{currency}-{investor_reference}. It must appear, unchanged, in the bank transfer reference field.
  • Money only moves between real bank accounts at deposit (step 1-2), disbursement (step 5), repayment (step 6), and withdrawal (step 8). Everything in between is internal bookkeeping in the ledger.
  • Every money movement is recorded as a balanced double-entry journal in the ledger, and every confirmed bank transfer is recorded as a "bank operation". These records are append-only: they are never edited or deleted, only added to or reversed with a new entry.

4. Money in BANXUM is not a bank account

This is the single most important idea for an operations admin. An investor's BANXUM balance is NOT a savings account.

The balance is non-interest-bearing operational money. "Non-interest-bearing" means simply holding money in BANXUM earns the investor nothing; interest only comes from funding loans. "Operational" means the balance exists so the investor can invest or withdraw, not to park cash. The funds are also segregated, meaning investor money is kept apart from Garanta's own money, not mixed with it.

Because BANXUM is not a deposit-taking bank, regulators do not allow investors to sit on idle cash indefinitely. So every balance lot is subject to ageing. Ageing means a parcel of money has clocks running against it from the day it was received, and what the investor is allowed to do with it changes as those clocks pass.

There are two ageing windows, measured from when the money was received:

The 30-day window (the investment deadline). For the first 30 days a lot is investable: it can be pledged into a published loan, or withdrawn. After 30 days that lot can no longer be used for new loan funding. It becomes withdraw-only. Important: the lot only has to be pledged while it is inside this 30-day window; the loan's funding campaign can close later, because published campaigns themselves are capped so the total holding time stays inside the 60-day operating limit.

The 60-day window (the withdrawal deadline). At 60 days the lot has reached the regulatory holding limit and must leave BANXUM. The investor cannot extend this deadline and neither can Garanta. The system sends the investor reminder emails as the deadline approaches (around days 25, 46, 53, 58, 59 and 60).

What happens when a lot reaches or passes the 60-day limit depends on whether the investor has given us a usable bank account to send the money to. "Usable" means a payout instruction (a registered IBAN) that an admin has verified. The handling fires on the first balance ageing scan at or after the deadline (the scan treats a lot as overdue once the business date is on or past the withdrawal deadline), so a lot already past 60 days is swept up too, not only one exactly on day 60. Two outcomes are possible, handled automatically by the balance ageing scan.

  • Forced withdrawal: if the investor HAS a verified, usable payout IBAN when a lot reaches or passes the 60-day limit, the system automatically creates a withdrawal request for that overdue money. "Forced" means the investor did not ask for it; the system raised it for them to honour the deadline. An admin then finalises the bank payout like any other withdrawal.
  • Penalty mode: if the investor has NO verified usable IBAN when a lot reaches or passes the 60-day limit, the system cannot send the money out, so the lot is moved into penalty mode. In plain words, a small daily charge starts eating into that specific balance (a per-day penalty in basis points, set as a deployment-level configuration value applied platform-wide; it is not a toggle you edit in the console). When any of the investor's money is in penalty mode, the investor app raises a blocking-severity "usable IBAN required" alert on their dashboard, prompting them to register a usable payout IBAN. Penalty-mode lots cannot be used for new investments, and the backend also blocks FX exchanges and secondary-market purchases while any penalty-mode balance exists. Withdrawal remains the intended exit path. If the daily charges ever consume the whole lot, it becomes penalty-exhausted (nothing left to charge).
  • Frozen: a separate balance lot status used when a lot is administratively held (for example during a compliance review). Frozen money still belongs to the investor but cannot be invested or withdrawn until the hold is lifted. Frozen is set deliberately, not by the ageing clock; penalty mode is what the 60-day clock produces when there is no usable IBAN.

5. The balance buckets

When you look at an investor's money, it is grouped into buckets. A bucket is just a label describing what the investor can currently do with that money. The bucket is decided per balance lot, from the lot's status and its two ageing clocks. The same investor can have money in several buckets at once.

Only money that is genuinely still sitting in the balance (the "available" amount of a lot) counts toward these buckets; money already invested, withdrawn, converted in an FX exchange, or eaten by penalties does not.

  • investable: available money younger than 30 days. The investor can pledge it into a loan OR withdraw it. This is the only bucket from which new loan investments can be made. The pledge must happen before the lot's 30-day investment deadline; the loan can still close funding later inside its own capped campaign window.
  • withdraw-only: available money past the 30-day investment deadline but not yet past the 60-day withdrawal deadline. It can no longer be invested; it can only be withdrawn.
  • overdue: available money past the 60-day withdrawal deadline. It is over the regulatory holding limit and must leave BANXUM. This is what the ageing scan turns into a forced withdrawal (if a usable IBAN exists) or penalty mode (if not).
  • frozen: money in a lot that has been administratively frozen. The investor can neither invest nor withdraw it until the freeze is removed.
  • penalty: money in a lot in penalty mode, being charged a daily penalty because it passed 60 days with no usable IBAN. Any penalty-mode balance raises a blocking-severity "usable IBAN required" alert in the investor app prompting them to fix their payout details. The penalty-mode lot itself is not investable, and current backend gates also block FX exchanges and secondary-market purchases until the situation is resolved. Withdrawal remains allowed so the money can leave the platform.
  • available (total): a roll-up of all of the above that still has money in it. It is the sum of every bucket; it is not a separate spendable pot of its own.

6. The life of a loan

A loan moves through a fixed set of statuses. Knowing the order tells you what is and is not allowed at each step.

draft: the loan exists only as an internal record an admin is preparing. Investors cannot see it. It can still be edited freely.

published: an admin has published the loan to the marketplace, so investors can now see it and invest. A loan can only be published from draft, and only when its required fields, repayment schedule, funding window and the borrower's KYB gate all pass. Once published it has a published_at timestamp.

funded: enough investor money has been committed and an admin closes funding. Only a funded loan can be disbursed to the borrower. (Funding can also be cancelled before close, returning reserved money to investors; a never-funded loan can end up cancelled.)

performing vs late vs defaulted: after disbursement the borrower repays on a schedule. As long as installments are paid on time the loan keeps performing (its status stays funded). If an installment is overdue by 5 days or more the system marks the loan late. If an installment is overdue by 16 days or more it is marked defaulted.

repaid: the borrower has paid everything back. The loan is complete and investors have received their principal and interest.

defaulted then recovery or written-off: a defaulted loan moves into recovery, where Garanta tries to collect what it can (for example by enforcing collateral). Money recovered is distributed to investors. If nothing more can be collected, the remaining amount is written off, meaning it is formally recognised as a loss to the investors who funded it.

Primary market vs secondary market, in one line each:

  • Primary market: where loans are funded for the first time. An investor places an investment order on a published loan, their balance is reserved and allocated, and when funding closes the money is committed and the borrower is disbursed. This is the normal way money goes into a loan.
  • Secondary market: where an investor who already holds a piece of a loan resells that holding to another investor before the loan finishes, to get their money out early. The seller lists the holding (sometimes at a discount or premium); a buyer pays for it; Garanta takes a fee from each side. Some listings publish automatically and some need admin approval.

7. Gates that protect everyone

BANXUM blocks risky or non-compliant actions by design. These blocks are called gates. The most important thing to understand: gates are enforced on the server (the backend), not just hidden in the screen. You cannot click your way past them in the admin console, and neither can an investor. If a gate is not satisfied, the action is refused server-side, full stop.

Identity gates. People are checked with KYC; companies are checked with KYB.

KYC (Know Your Customer) is identity and anti-money-laundering verification for a person, run through Didit. A KYC case has a status, and only one status, "approved", lets the person act. Every other status blocks them, including not_started, pending, declined, manual_review, high_risk, sanctions_hit, pep_hit, adverse_media_hit, expired, and reverification_required. A "sanctions hit" means the person matched a sanctions list; a "PEP hit" means they were flagged as a Politically Exposed Person (someone in or close to public office, who needs extra scrutiny). When Didit's automated result is unclear or flagged, the case goes to manual_review, where a Garanta compliance admin decides to approve, decline, or request re-verification.

KYB (Know Your Business) is the company equivalent, for borrowers. A borrower can only transact (receive a disbursement) when its KYB status is "approved" AND it has no compliance hold. A "compliance hold" is a manual switch an admin can set to stop a borrower for any reason, regardless of KYB status.

Phone verification. Sensitive money actions also require the investor's phone number to be verified. An account is phone-verified only once a verification challenge has been confirmed.

Account statuses. Every account carries a status: pending_kyc (the default for a brand-new account that has not finished verification), active, restricted, locked, or closed. Pending-KYC lender accounts may still log in so onboarding can resume, but they cannot use financial features yet. Restricted, locked, and closed accounts cannot log in; closing also makes the account inactive.

Putting it together for investors: the system only lets an investor reach financial features (balances, investing, withdrawing) when the account is an active lender AND the phone is verified. There is one more gate that depends on the account type. For a natural-person lender ("natural_person_lender"), that person must also have an approved KYC case. For a company representative ("legal_entity_lender_representative"), active-lender plus phone-verified is enough; there is no personal-KYC check on the representative, because the company's identity is handled through the entity's KYB instead. Miss a required gate and the financial screens are refused.

  • Approved is the only green light. For KYC and for KYB, every status other than "approved" is a stop.
  • Compliance hold overrides KYB: a borrower with approved KYB but an active compliance hold still cannot receive money.
  • Gates live in the backend. The console will not offer an override button, because the rule is enforced where the money actually moves.

8. What the admin console is for

The console at /admin is organised into seven areas in the left-hand navigation. Here is each one in a single sentence so you know where to go.

  • Daily dashboard: your morning overview, showing the work queues that need attention today (KYC reviews, bank exceptions, withdrawals, forced withdrawals, balance ageing, funding loans, servicing, loan risk, secondary approvals, FX settlement, failed emails, reconciliation breaks).
  • Tasks: the admin task list, where operational to-dos are tracked and worked through.
  • Compliance: where you review KYC/KYB cases, work the manual-review queue, and act on sanctions, PEP, and adverse-media flags coming from Didit.
  • Finance ops: where money is handled, confirming deposits and bank operations, finalising withdrawals (including forced ones), running the balance ageing scan, handling FX (currency exchange) settlement, and doing reconciliation against the bank.
  • Loans: where you create and edit borrowers and loans, publish loans to the marketplace, and close or cancel funding.
  • Reports: where you generate operational and financial reports (for example FX delta and realised-settlement reports) for a chosen date range.
  • Superadmin settings: superadmin-only configuration, such as document templates and platform settings, and creating other admin accounts.

Getting into the console

The admin console lives at the /admin address of the BANXUM web app. It is completely separate from the investor app that lenders use. Only staff accounts of type admin or superadmin, with an active status, can sign in.

Signing in is two steps. First you enter your work email and password. Then BANXUM emails you a one-time numeric code, which you type on the next screen. This second step (an emailed code) is a security measure — even if someone learns your password they cannot get in without your mailbox. If you mistype the code too many times you must start again.

The layout. A dark navigation rail runs down the left with the seven areas of the console (described in the sections that follow). The main area on the right shows the selected area. A top bar shows where you are and who the operator is.

Preview vs. live data. When the console shows a small "Preview data" tag near the top right, you are looking at fixture (demo) data — safe for training and screenshots, not real customers. A live deployment has no such tag and every action affects real money and real people. The screenshots in this manual were taken in preview mode, so the names and numbers are invented.

Signing out. Use the "Sign out" button at the bottom of the left rail when you finish. Never leave an authenticated admin session unattended.

Section 3

Daily Dashboard

The Daily Dashboard is the first screen you see when you open the ops console, and it is the screen you start each shift on. Think of it as a control tower: it gathers the work that needs human attention from every part of the platform into one place, so you can see at a glance what is waiting and how urgent it is. (The ops console is the internal admin tool used by Garanta staff; it is separate from the app that investors and borrowers use.)

The screen has three parts. At the top is a row of four KPI tiles (KPI means 'key performance indicator' - a headline number you watch). Below that is the queues area: a left rail listing 13 work QUEUES, each with a live count, and a table showing the items inside whichever queue you have selected. At the bottom is a multi-currency balance summary table that shows the platform's money position broken down by currency (for example CHF and EUR).

A QUEUE is simply a named list of things that need looking at - for example loans waiting for funding, or investor withdrawals waiting to be paid out. Each item in a queue points at a real record elsewhere in the platform (a loan, a withdrawal, a bank transfer, and so on).

Important: this screen is read-only triage. You use it to SEE and PRIORITISE work, not to act on it. To actually approve a KYC case, pay a withdrawal, publish a loan, and so on, you go to the matching module screen in the left navigation (Compliance, Finance ops, Loans, etc.). Clicking a queue item here opens a detail drawer that shows context only; it does not yet perform any action.

What you do on this screen
  • Read the four KPI tiles to gauge the overall load: how many items are open, how many are high priority, how many are past their deadline, and how many touch money.
  • Scan the 13 queue tabs in the left rail and note the count badge on each - a non-zero count means there is work waiting in that area.
  • Select a queue to load its table, then read each row's status, priority, due date and amount to decide what to handle first.
  • Spot overdue items: rows that are past their due date (and not already finished) are highlighted so you can escalate them.
  • Open an item's detail drawer to read its full context and metadata before you switch to the relevant module to act.
  • Read the bottom currency table to understand the platform's money position per currency - what is investable, what is locked, what is overdue, and what is pending in bank or FX operations.
  • On the Reconciliation breaks queue only, use the Create tasks button to turn open reconciliation exceptions into trackable admin tasks; use Refresh to reload the latest numbers.
Section 4

Tasks

The Tasks screen is the operations team's shared to-do list. A "task" is one piece of work an admin needs to do, for example "resolve an unmatched bank deposit" or "review a KYC case". KYC means Know Your Customer: the legal checks that confirm who a person is before they can use a financial service. The screen lets you create tasks, sort through them, and move each one along until it is finished.

Tasks come from two places. Most are created by hand by an admin (the New task button). Some are created automatically by the system, such as a "reconciliation break" task. Reconciliation is the daily check that the money the bank says we hold matches the money our own records say we hold; a "break" is a mismatch that someone must investigate. Auto-generated tasks show a specific Type (for example Payment Reconciliation) and are linked to the record that caused them.

Each task has a Type, a Priority (how urgent), a Status (where it is in its life), an optional Due date, and an optional link to a real object in the system (a loan, an investor, a withdrawal, and so on). You can link a task to that object so anyone reading it knows exactly what it is about.

Important: this screen is a lightweight tracker only. It does not perform the actual money or compliance actions. The real work (executing a withdrawal, approving a loan, recording a KYC decision) is done on the owning screen (Finance ops, Loans, Compliance). A task is just a reminder and an audit trail that the work happened.

What you do on this screen
  • Create a new internal task with a title, type, priority, optional due date, notes, and an optional link to a real object.
  • Filter the task list by status, priority, or type, and free-text search across title, notes, and object id.
  • Open any task to see its full detail, edit its fields, and read its event history.
  • Move a task through its statuses (Open, In progress, Waiting, Resolved, Cancelled) using quick-action buttons.
  • Tell apart manually-created tasks from auto-generated ones (such as reconciliation breaks) by their Type and linked object.
  • Record a completion note when you resolve or cancel a task, so the reason is captured permanently.
Section 5

Compliance

Compliance is where Garanta staff make the identity and money-laundering decisions that an automated check could not finish on its own.

Some background terms, in plain language. KYC means "Know Your Customer": proving a person really is who they say they are. KYB is the same idea for a business ("Know Your Business"). AML means "Anti-Money-Laundering": checking that a customer is not on a sanctions list, is not a known criminal, and is not trying to hide where their money came from.

BANXUM does not run these checks by hand. An outside provider called Didit collects the customer's ID documents and screens them. Didit then returns a result. Most results are automatic: clearly approved, or clearly declined. But some results are unclear and Didit "routes them to manual review", which means it hands the case to a Garanta officer to decide. Those cases land on this screen.

A "PEP" is a Politically Exposed Person: someone in a powerful public role (or close to one), who is treated as higher-risk. "Adverse media" means bad news coverage linking the person to crime or wrongdoing. "Sanctions" are official government blocklists; a person on one is legally forbidden to do business.

The second half of this screen is unrelated to KYC: it lets an admin change a user's account status, for example to freeze a suspicious account or close one on request.

What you do on this screen
  • Read the cases Didit sent to manual review and see why each was flagged (PEP, high risk, adverse media, and so on).
  • Record an AML decision on a selected case: approve it, decline it, ask the customer to re-verify, or reopen it for further review.
  • Change a user account's status: restrict, lock, close, or reactivate it, always with a reason and a note.
  • Understand the hard limits the system enforces for you: sanctions hits and provider declines can never be approved by hand, closing an account needs a clean-account confirmation, and only a superadmin may touch another admin's account.
Section 6

Finance Operations

Finance Operations is the money-movement cockpit of the admin console. This is where Garanta staff record cash that has actually entered or left the company's bank account, and where the system's internal ledger is kept in step with reality. Nothing here moves money by itself: each form records, in the ledger, a bank transfer that you (or the bank) have already arranged or observed.

A few terms you will see on every form. 'Minor units' means the amount in the smallest currency unit, i.e. cents (rappen for CHF). So 2,500,000 minor units equals 25,000.00 CHF. Always enter whole numbers in minor units, never decimals. The 'collection account' is Garanta's own pooled bank account that holds all lender money before it is invested or paid out. A 'deposit' is incoming money from an investor; a 'disbursement' is outgoing money to a borrower; a 'withdrawal' is outgoing money back to an investor.

Most forms include an 'investor' (or loan/borrower/withdrawal) autocomplete. You type a name, email, investor reference (a code like L7K3M9PQ2), UUID, or IBAN; the box searches the database and lets you pick a real record. When you pick one, the box quietly stores the matching hidden ID and shows 'Selected ID: ...'. If you type text but never pick a result, the form will refuse to submit. The visible text is just for searching; the hidden ID is what actually gets sent.

Because each action posts permanent accounting entries, every submit carries an automatic idempotency key so an accidental double-click cannot double-book a transfer. Watch the success and error banners after each action: they tell you exactly what the ledger did.

What you do on this screen
  • Declare a matched incoming lender deposit and credit it to the investor's balance.
  • Register the verified IBAN (payout instruction) Garanta will use to pay an investor.
  • Look up an investor's ledger balance broken into investable, withdraw-only, overdue, frozen and penalty buckets.
  • Run the balance ageing scan that fires 30/60-day reminders, forced withdrawals, and penalty charges (with a safe dry-run mode).
  • Create a reconciliation snapshot that compares the bank's stated balance against what the ledger expects.
  • Finalize or cancel investor withdrawals, finalize borrower loan disbursements, and declare external FX (currency-exchange) settlements.
Section 7

Loans & Marketplace

This screen is the lending engine of BANXUM. It is where you set up the companies that borrow money, build the loans they want, and run those loans from the moment they go live until they are fully repaid or written off.

A quick mental model of the product. Individual investors (called lenders) put money into their BANXUM account. The platform then funds loans to vetted business borrowers. Borrowers repay over time with interest. Lenders earn that interest and can later withdraw.

Borrowers are companies, not people with logins. There is no borrower portal. An admin types in every borrower's details by hand. The borrower never sees this screen.

Two jargon terms you will see everywhere. The primary market is where a brand-new loan is first funded by investors. The secondary market is where an investor who already owns a slice of a loan sells that slice to another investor. This screen handles admin tasks for both.

This page is laid out top to bottom as: key metrics, then a Borrowers table, then a Loans table, then four action panels (Primary marketplace operations, Secondary-market approvals, and Servicing and recovery).

What you do on this screen
  • Add and edit borrower companies, and watch their KYB status (the company-level identity and compliance check).
  • Create loan drafts, edit them, and review their status, amount, interest rate, LTV and funding deadline.
  • Publish a loan to the marketplace so investors can fund it, then close or cancel its funding, run an expiry scan, or release a stuck investor order.
  • Approve, reject or remove non-standard secondary-market listings (investor-to-investor resales).
  • Service live loans: record borrower repayments, run a status scan that flags late or defaulted loans, publish public notes to investors, and record recovery payments on defaulted loans.
Section 8

Reports & Audit

This screen has two jobs. On the left you generate exports: data files that pull together accounting, tax, regulatory, and day-to-day operational records into a single download. On the right you search the audit log: a permanent record of who did what, to what, and when.

A "report" here is just a structured snapshot of platform data for a chosen date range, produced as a file you can hand to accountants, auditors, regulators, or banks. You pick what data you want (the report type), the file format (CSV, PDF, or ZIP), how much sensitive detail to include (the redaction mode), and the time period. The system builds the file on demand and gives it back to you to download.

The audit log is the platform's tamper-proof history book. Every meaningful action by staff or the system is written here once and can never be edited or deleted (it is "append-only"). It records the actor (who), the action (what they did), the target (what it was done to), and the exact time. It is your evidence trail when someone asks "who changed this?".

Important context for a newcomer: PII means "personally identifiable information" such as names and account-owner IDs. Some reports and the "Full" redaction mode expose this sensitive data, so the system restricts them to senior staff (superadmins). Generating any report itself writes an entry into the audit log, so report generation is never invisible.

What you do on this screen
  • Generate an accounting, tax, regulatory, or operational export by choosing a report type, output format, redaction mode, and date range, then clicking Generate report.
  • Choose how much sensitive detail the export contains using the redaction mode (Redacted hides personal identifiers; Full shows everything and is superadmin-only).
  • Set the reporting period either with a quick preset (daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly, yearly, calendar year) or with custom start and end dates.
  • Download the finished file, and verify its integrity using the SHA-256 checksum the system records for each run.
  • Search and review the immutable audit log to see who performed which action against which target, and exactly when it happened.

Handle a day-60 forced withdrawal

Goal: Return funds that an investor has held idle past the 60-day regulatory limit, then pay them out.  ·  Who: Operations admin (finance).

Before you start:
  • Jargon: 'balance lot' = a dated chunk of an investor's money; each lot's ageing is tracked from the deposit's VALUE DATE (the value_date entered on the deposit), not literally the day it arrived. 'Ageing' = how old a lot is. The platform must not hold uninvested money beyond 60 days. 'Forced withdrawal' = the platform automatically returning over-age funds to the investor. 'Penalty mode' = what happens to an over-age lot when there is NO verified payout IBAN to send it to: the lot is moved to penalty mode AND a real per-day penalty (rate BALANCE_PENALTY_BPS_PER_DAY) is charged against the lot's available balance every time the scan runs while it stays overdue. Penalty mode also blocks FX exchanges and secondary-market purchases until resolved; withdrawal remains the intended way out. Reminder days fire at 25, 46, 53, 58, 59, 60 days held.
  • Investors with over-age lots need a verified payout instruction on file (see the payout-IBAN flow) for a forced withdrawal to be created automatically.
  1. Daily dashboard
    On the dashboard, open the 'Balance ageing' and 'Forced withdrawals' queues to see what is near or past 60 days.
    'Balance ageing' lists lots near or past the 30/60-day windows; 'Forced withdrawals' lists day-60 return-of-funds withdrawals already generated by the ageing scan.
  2. Finance ops > Balance ageing scan
    Open Finance ops, go to 'Balance ageing scan'. First run it as a DRY RUN (leave 'Dry run only' ticked) to preview.
    Optionally set 'As of timestamp' and a 'Currency filter'. A dry run reports how many reminders are due and how many forced withdrawals WOULD be created, without changing anything.
  3. Finance ops > Balance ageing scan
    When the preview looks right, untick 'Dry run only' and click 'Run ageing scan' for real.
    GATE / BEHAVIOR: for each investor whose lots are past the withdrawal deadline, the scan checks for an active VERIFIED payout instruction. If one exists, it CREATES a forced withdrawal request (status 'requested') for the over-age amount. If NONE exists, the lot instead moves to 'penalty mode' and no withdrawal is created - and on this and every subsequent live scan while still overdue, the scan charges a real daily penalty (rate BALANCE_PENALTY_BPS_PER_DAY) against the lot's balance, recording a journal entry each time. So registering a verified IBAN (see the payout-IBAN flow) and re-running is TIME-SENSITIVE, not just bookkeeping.
  4. Finance ops > Withdrawal execution
    Go to 'Withdrawal execution', pick the newly generated forced withdrawal in the 'Requested withdrawal' lookup, send the bank transfer, then click 'Finalize withdrawal'.
    A forced withdrawal is executed exactly like a normal one - it lives in the same 'requested' withdrawal queue. Enter Booking date, Value date, Collection account, and finalize after the money has been sent.

Result: Over-age funds leave the platform as finalized withdrawals; lots with no verified IBAN are moved to penalty mode and are actively charged a daily penalty against their balance on every live scan until a verified IBAN is registered.

Watch out for
  • Always dry-run first. The live scan writes reminders and creates real withdrawal requests.
  • If a forced withdrawal was NOT created for someone over 60 days, they almost certainly lack a verified payout instruction - and their over-age lot is now in penalty mode being charged a daily penalty on each live scan. Register a verified IBAN and re-run promptly; the delay costs the investor money.
  • The 60-day clock is per-lot, not per-account; a new deposit does not reset an older lot. The clock runs from each lot's VALUE DATE, so back- or forward-dating a deposit's value date shifts that lot's whole ageing timeline.

'defaulted' = at least 16 days past due. 'status scan' = a job that re-checks each loan's due dates and moves it to late/defaulted as appropriate. 'recovery' = collecting money on a defaulted loan. 'risk note' = a public update to affected investors.

Generate a regulatory report and find an action in the audit log

Before you start:
  • Jargon: 'report type' = the kind of export (e.g. Operational Subledger, Trial Balance, Garanta Accrued Revenue, Bexio Accounting Export, Bank Operations). 'redaction mode' = Redacted (personal data hidden) or Full. 'period preset' = Custom (you set start/end) or a rolling window (Daily/Weekly/Monthly/Quarterly/Yearly/Calendar Year). Section 11

Glossary & quick reference

Key terms

P2P lending (peer-to-peer lending)
The core business. Many individual investors put up the money to fund a loan to a business borrower. The borrower repays the loan with interest over time. Investors earn that interest. BANXUM is the marketplace that connects the two sides and handles the money and paperwork.
BANXUM
The brand name of the marketplace platform. It is operated by Garanta Finanzgruppe AG, a Swiss financial group. You work inside its internal admin console at /admin.
Garanta Finanzgruppe AG
The Swiss company that runs BANXUM. In the system, money and fee accounts owned by the operator are labelled "Garanta" (for example Garanta accrued revenue, Garanta in, Garanta out).
Lender / Investor
A person or company that deposits money and uses it to fund loans, then earns interest. "Lender" and "investor" mean the same thing here. An account can be a natural-person lender (an individual) or a legal-entity lender represented by a person.
Borrower
A vetted business that receives loan money and repays it with interest. Borrowers are companies (called borrower entities), not individuals. Examples of entity types: Swiss company, non-Swiss company, real estate project company, special purpose vehicle.
KYC (Know Your Customer)
The identity and background checks done on a person before they are allowed to use money on the platform. It confirms who they are and screens them against risk lists. Used for lenders/investors. A lender who has not passed KYC cannot transact.
KYB (Know Your Business)
The same idea as KYC but for a company (a borrower). It checks the business is real, who owns it, who runs it, and whether it is safe to deal with. A borrower can only transact when KYB is approved and there is no compliance hold.
AML (Anti-Money-Laundering)
The set of legal checks that stop the platform being used to launder illegal money. KYC and KYB screening feed into AML. Reviews are often labelled "KYC/AML".
Didit
The outside vendor (third-party provider) that runs the automated identity-verification and screening checks. Most KYC cases use Didit. When Didit cannot give a clear answer, the case is routed to Garanta staff for manual review.
Manual review
A KYC/KYB case the automated provider could not decide on its own, so a human admin must make the call. Reasons include PEP review, high-risk review, adverse-media review, sanctions review, provider decline, inconclusive provider result, or a document/identity issue. The admin can approve, decline, request re-verification, or reopen the review.
Sanctions hit
Screening found the person or business on an official sanctions list (people/entities that the law forbids doing business with). This blocks the account until reviewed.
PEP (Politically Exposed Person)
Someone in a senior public role, or close to one (for example a government minister or their family). They are not banned, but they need extra scrutiny because of higher corruption risk. A PEP hit triggers manual review.
Adverse media
Negative news found about the person or business (for example reports of fraud or crime). An adverse-media hit triggers manual review before the account can proceed.
Compliance hold
A manual flag on a borrower that blocks it from transacting, even if its KYB is otherwise approved. Used when compliance staff need to pause activity.
Primary market
Where brand-new loans are funded for the first time. A loan is published, investors commit money to it, and once it is fully funded the money goes to the borrower. This is the original sale of the loan.
Secondary market
Where an investor who already holds a piece of a loan can sell that holding to another investor before the loan finishes. The original loan does not change; only who owns the right to its future repayments changes.
Holding
An investor's stake in a specific loan: the share of the principal they funded and are owed repayments on. A holding is the thing that gets sold on the secondary market.
Maker / Taker
On the secondary market, the maker is the seller who lists a holding for sale; the taker is the buyer who purchases it. Each pays a separate fee (maker fee, taker fee).
Transfer price
The amount the buyer pays the seller for a secondary-market holding. It can be at a discount or premium to the outstanding principal (shown in basis points), plus any accrued interest.
Accrued interest
Interest that has built up on a loan but has not yet been paid out. On a secondary sale, the buyer compensates the seller for interest earned up to the sale date.
Balance lot
A single tracked batch of an investor's money, recorded separately by where it came from (a deposit, a repayment installment, a recovery distribution, secondary-market proceeds, FX proceeds, a refund, or a correction). The platform tracks each lot's own age and how much is still available, invested, converted, withdrawn, or penalized. Money is used oldest-lot-first.
Minor units
Money is stored as whole numbers of the smallest currency unit (cents/centimes) to avoid rounding errors. A field ending in "_minor" holds minor units. Example: 150000 minor units = CHF 1,500.00. The console formats these into normal amounts for display.
Basis points (bps)
A unit for rates and fees. 100 bps = 1%. So an interest rate of 850 bps means 8.50%. Used for interest rates, fees, discounts/premiums, and loan-to-value.
LTV (loan-to-value)
How big the loan is compared with the value of the collateral backing it, shown in basis points. Lower is safer. A warning is raised if collateral value is zero or smaller than the loan.
Collateral
Assets a borrower pledges to back a loan, so investors can be repaid if the borrower fails. Types include real estate, corporate or personal guarantees, receivables, invoices, equipment, inventory, securities or share pledges, and cash.
Risk rating
A credit grade for a loan, from AAA (safest) down through B and C grades to D, or "unrated". It signals how risky the loan is to investors.
Principal
The original loan amount borrowed, before interest. "Committed principal" is how much investors have pledged so far toward funding it.
Investor reference
A short unique code (up to 10 characters) automatically assigned to each lender account. It identifies the investor across the system and is unique per lender.
Payment reference
A code written on a bank transfer so the platform can match incoming or outgoing money to the right deposit, withdrawal, or settlement. Stored on bank operations and withdrawal records.
Collection account
The platform's own bank account where investor money and repayments are collected and held. Bank operations record which collection account a transfer touched.
Bank operation
A recorded movement of real money in or out of the platform's bank, such as a lender deposit, lender withdrawal, borrower disbursement, borrower repayment, or an FX settlement. Each must be matched (reconciled) to the internal ledger.
Ledger
The platform's internal double-entry bookkeeping system. Every money event creates a journal entry with balanced debit and credit postings across accounts (for example collection cash, investor balance liability, escrow, fee revenue).
Payout instruction (IBAN)
An investor's saved bank-account details (IBAN and account name) used to send their withdrawals. There can be only one active payout instruction per investor per currency. An admin must verify it before it can be used.
IBAN
International Bank Account Number: the standard format for identifying a bank account when sending money. It is the destination for an investor's payout.
Withdrawal
An investor's request to take their available money out of the platform and have it sent to their bank account. Garanta executes the bank transfer, then marks the request finalized.
Forced withdrawal
A withdrawal the platform starts on its own (not requested by the investor) to return money that has sat uninvested too long. Triggered by balance ageing at the day-60 window for regulatory reasons. Marked with the "is_forced" flag.
Ageing
Tracking how long each balance lot of uninvested money has been sitting. Swiss rules limit how long the platform may hold idle investor cash, so lots approaching the 30-day and 60-day windows surface for action, and day-60 lots get a forced withdrawal back to the investor.
Disbursement
Paying out the loan money to the borrower once a loan is fully funded. Held first in a loan funding escrow account, then released to the borrower (borrower disbursement).
Escrow
A holding account where committed funding money sits temporarily between the moment investors commit it and the moment it is disbursed to the borrower.
Servicing
The ongoing management of a live loan: tracking the repayment schedule, collecting each installment from the borrower on its due date, and distributing repayments to investors.
Installment
One scheduled repayment in a loan's repayment plan, with its own due date and split between principal and interest. The full set of installments is the repayment schedule.
Recovery
Efforts to get money back from a borrower that has stopped paying (is late or in default), for example by enforcing the collateral. Money clawed back is distributed to investors through a recovery distribution following a defined waterfall (order of who gets paid first).
Default
A loan where the borrower has failed to repay as agreed for long enough to be declared in default. It moves into recovery work and may eventually be written off.
Write-off
Formally recording that a loan's outstanding amount is treated as a loss because it is judged unrecoverable.
Reconciliation
Checking that the platform's internal ledger matches the real bank balances and that investor balances add up. Done by comparing a reconciliation snapshot (bank balance vs. ledger figures) on a given date.
Reconciliation break
A mismatch found during reconciliation: the ledger and bank disagree, a balance has the wrong sign, or an investor's balance fails an integrity check. Breaks must be investigated and resolved.
Suspense / unmatched cash
Money that has arrived in or left the bank but cannot yet be linked to a specific deposit, repayment, or instruction. It is parked in a suspense account until an admin matches it.
Outbox email
Transactional system emails (login codes, confirmations, notices) are queued in an outbox before being sent, so delivery can be tracked and retried.
Dead-letter email
An email that failed to send after the system gave up retrying. It lands in a dead-letter state and shows in the Failed emails queue for an admin to review.
FX (foreign exchange / currency exchange)
Converting an investor's balance from one currency to another inside the platform. The investor gets a quote (rate plus fee), and on acceptance an exchange is executed, producing balance in the new currency.
FX quote
A priced, time-limited offer to convert one currency to another at a given rate, with the platform fee shown. It expires if not used and passes a sanity check against the previous day's average rate.
FX settlement (external settlement)
Internal currency conversions are netted and then actually settled with a real bank in the outside market. An admin declares the external settlement: the real amounts sold and bought, the actual rate, and the linked bank operations. Until declared, internal FX shows up in the FX settlement queue.
Audit event
An append-only (never edited or deleted) record of who did what and when. The system logs these for account-status changes, loan changes, KYC/KYB decisions, secondary-market listing changes, and FX actions, capturing the actor, before/after status, reason, and notes.
Append-only
A record type that can be added to but never changed or removed, so the history stays trustworthy. Most event and ledger records are append-only.
Idempotency key
A unique tag attached to a money operation so that, if the same request is sent twice, it is only processed once. Prevents accidental double deposits, withdrawals, or postings.
Sensitive action
A higher-risk operation that requires an extra one-time confirmation code, such as admin login, a withdrawal, a bank-account change, an FX exchange, a primary investment, or a secondary-market listing or purchase.
Reverification
Asking a customer to redo their identity/business verification, for example because the previous one expired or new information is needed. The case status becomes "re-verification required".
